Description

Driven by Dubai and Abu Dhabi, the UAE has been profoundly entrenched in the contemporary foreign influence, politics and policy-making framework. Only an independent state since 1971, the seven emirates that make up the UAE constitute not only the most productive federal Arab experiment but also the most enduring one. The financial crisis of 2008 and its aftermath, however, underlined the ongoing imbalance between Abu Dhabi and Dubai and the five northern emirates. Meanwhile, the post-2011 security crackdown has shown that officials in Abu Dhabi are highly responsive to social inequality and economic disparities throughout the federation.

The United Arab Emirates: Power, Politics, and Policymaking charts the various processes of state formation and political and economic development that have enabled the UAE to emerge as a significant regional power and a major player in the post Arab Spring reordering of the Middle East and North African Politics, as well as the closest partner of the US in military and security affairs in the region. It also examines the seamier underside of the development in terms of migrant workers ‘plight, recent interventions in Libya and Yemen, and, latterly, one of the world’s highest rates of per capita political prisoners. The book concludes with a discussion of the possible policy challenges facing the UAE in the coming years, especially as it moves towards its 50th anniversary in 2021.

This book will be a valuable tool for students and scholars in International Relations and Middle East Studies, as well as non-specialists with an interest in the United Arab Emirates and its global role, offering a detailed and open evaluation of the UAE.
